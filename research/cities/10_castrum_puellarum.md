> **Updated identification (from Bathemolin research)**: The orient-latin.com fortress database identifies Castrum Puellarum with **Sari Seki** — a Templar fortress at the northwestern approach to the Syrian Gates, controlling a coastal passage called "La Portella." This fits the geographic ordering perfectly: the list proceeds from Roche Roissol → Castrum Puellarum (Sari Seki) → Bathemolin (coastal road) → Alexandretta — all along the Amanus coastal route. The earlier Euphrates identification (Qal'at al-Banat) is geographically implausible given the surrounding entries.

## 1. Identity & Status

If the Sari Seki identification is correct (now the preferred reading), this was a **Templar fortress at the NW approach to the Syrian Gates**, controlling a narrow coastal passage ("La Portella") between the castle and the sea — the southern entrance to the Cilician plain. It was part of the same Amanus pass defense system as Baghras and Trapessac.

The alternative Euphrates identification (Qal'at al-Banat, partially submerged by Lake Assad) is now considered less likely given the geographic ordering of the cities list.

## 2. Strategic Importance

**Impact: MEDIUM-HIGH** (if Euphrates identification is correct)

- **Military role**: Castles on the Euphrates controlled **river crossings** — the fundamental strategic asset of the County of Edessa, which straddled the river. Control of crossings determined whether reinforcements, trade, and communication could flow between Edessa proper (east bank) and the Frankish states to the west.
- **Political role**: The Euphrates line was the lifeline of the County of Edessa. Losing crossings meant the county could be cut in two.
- Secondary to the major crossings at **Birecik (Birta)** and **Qal'at Ja'bar**, but part of the same defensive network.

## 3. Geography & Nature

- **Terrain**: The Euphrates valley in this region is a broad, relatively flat river plain with bluffs on either side. Castles were built on prominent bluffs or headlands overlooking the river.
- **Euphrates River**: The defining geographic feature. The river was a major transportation artery.
- **Balikh River**: Joins the Euphrates from the north near Raqqa (downstream).
- Upstream: **Qal'at Ja'bar** (another major Euphrates fortress), and further north the crossing at **Birecik**.
- **Agriculture**: HIGH. The Euphrates valley was highly productive irrigated agricultural land — grain, cotton, fruit orchards. The river provided water, fish, and transportation.

## 4. Trade & Economy

- **Trade routes**: The Euphrates was a major trade artery connecting Mesopotamia (Baghdad, Mosul) with the Mediterranean world. River traffic and parallel overland routes were commercially vital.
- **Commodities**: Grain, cotton, silk, spices (in transit from the east), livestock.
- **Market significance**: Medium — derived from controlling a river crossing rather than being a major market in its own right.

## 5. Military Conflicts (1099–1147)

- **1098–1100** — Baldwin of Boulogne established the County of Edessa, securing Euphrates crossings.
- **1104 — Battle of Harran**: Devastating Frankish defeat east of the Euphrates. The county lost significant territory and control over some Euphrates positions weakened.
- **1108–1110s** — Ongoing conflicts over Euphrates crossings and fortifications.
- **1122** — Joscelin I captured or secured several Euphrates positions.
- **1131** — Joscelin I died; his son Joscelin II was less militarily capable.
- **1144 — Fall of Edessa**: Zengi captured Edessa itself. Euphrates fortifications progressively lost or isolated.
- **1146** — Joscelin II briefly recaptured Edessa but was driven out. Remaining Euphrates positions crumbled.

## 6. Key Characters

- **Baldwin I of Jerusalem** (Count of Edessa 1098–1100): Established Frankish control of Euphrates crossings.
- **Baldwin II** (Count 1100–1118): Consolidated the county's Euphrates positions.
- **Joscelin I of Courtenay** (Count 1119–1131): Most effective defender of the county; secured Euphrates positions.
- **Joscelin II** (Count 1131–1150): Presided over the county's decline and loss of Euphrates fortifications.
- **Imad ad-Din Zengi**: Captured Edessa 1144, triggering the Second Crusade.

## Design Notes

If the Euphrates identification is correct, this is a strategically important site controlling a river crossing — the lifeline of the County of Edessa. Game mechanics should reflect: (1) river crossing control, (2) connection to both sides of the Euphrates, (3) vulnerability to being cut off when the county contracts. However, the geographic ordering in the cities list (between frontier castles near Antioch) raises the possibility this refers to a different, more northerly site. Worth double-checking against Cahen's maps.
