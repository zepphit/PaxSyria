# Hion (Hien) Sinab

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Frankish name** | Hion Sinab, Hien Sinab |
| **Probable Arabic name** | Hisn Sinab (حصن سناب) — "Fortress of Sinab" |
| **Modern name** | Uncertain — likely in Kilis Province, Turkey |
| **Approx. location** | Between Ravanda (Ravendel) and Nebi Huri (Cyrrhus), in the hilly corridor of the Kilis/Gaziantep border region |

> **Note on identification**: The prefix "Hion/Hien" almost certainly derives from Arabic **Hisn** (حصن, "fortress"), a very common element in Crusader-era place names. "Hisn Sinab" = "Fortress of Sinab." The exact modern identification is uncertain — this is a minor fortification that likely appears in only one or two medieval sources. Claude Cahen's *La Syrie du Nord* (1940) is the key reference for sites of this class.

## 1. Identity & Status

A small fortified site in the chain of defensive positions along the route between Ravendel and Cyrrhus. This corridor connected the County of Edessa's western territories with the Principality of Antioch.

### Control timeline

| Period | Controller | Notes |
|--------|-----------|-------|
| ~1098–1144 | County of Edessa (western portion) | Part of the defensive network; subordinate to the Joscelins |
| After 1144 | Abandoned or fell to Zengi/Nur ad-Din | Fall of Edessa made this corridor untenable |

## 2. Strategic Importance

**Impact: LOW**

- **Military role**: Minor fortress in a chain of defensive positions along the Ravendel-Cyrrhus corridor. This corridor was important because it connected the County of Edessa with the Principality of Antioch.
- **Political role**: Negligible as an independent entity. Purely a garrison point. Local castellans are unrecorded.

## 3. Geography & Nature

- **Terrain**: Hilly, semi-arid limestone country with seasonal wadis. Rocky ridges suitable for small fortifications overlooking passes and routes.
- **Afrin River valley**: Lies to the west.
- **Agriculture**: Possible in valley bottoms — olives, grain, some livestock. Modest winter rainfall.
- The numerous tells and ruined fortifications in this corridor attest to its long history as a defended route.

## 4. Trade & Economy

- Sat along a secondary route connecting Aleppo's economic hinterland (via Cyrrhus and Azaz) with territories further north and east.
- Trade would have been local and agricultural — grain, olive oil, livestock.
- No evidence of significant commercial activity at this specific site.

## 5. Military Conflicts (1099–1147)

No specific sieges or battles recorded at this site by name. However, the corridor was repeatedly contested:

- **1110s** — Repeated Muslim raids from Aleppo into Edessan territory.
- **1119 — Field of Blood**: Destabilized the entire northern frontier; sites like this would have been at risk.
- **1122–1124** — Joscelin I's conflicts with Balak of Mardin affected this region.
- **1144** — Fall of Edessa made this corridor untenable. Likely abandoned or fell without recorded siege.

## 6. Key Characters

- **Joscelin I of Courtenay** (Count of Edessa, 1119–1131): Overlord of this territory.
- **Joscelin II of Courtenay** (Count of Edessa, 1131–1150): Under his watch the county collapsed.
- Local castellans unrecorded.

## Design Notes

Very minor site — best represented as part of a frontier chain rather than a standalone location. A tower or small castle with a garrison of a few dozen men, controlling a road segment. Its game value is in representing the network of minor fortifications that made Crusader territorial control possible.
