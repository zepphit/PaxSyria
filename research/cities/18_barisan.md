# Barisan

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Frankish name** | Barisan, Barissan |
| **Arabic name** | Barsha (برشا) or Barisha (باريشا) |
| **Modern name** | Barisha, in the Jebel Barisha / Jebel Sem'an region, NW Syria |
| **Approx. location** | Limestone massif ("Dead Cities") between the Afrin valley (NE) and the plain of Antioch (S/SW) |

> **Note on identification**: "Barisan" as a place name is easily confused with the famous Barisan of Ibelin (Crusader dynasty). Here the geographic context — between Afrin and the Antioch plain — points firmly to **Barisha** in the Jebel Barisha limestone massif. The identification is geographically plausible but not confirmed in a primary Crusader-era source naming "Barisan" as this specific site.

## 1. Identity & Status

Barisha sits in the "Dead Cities" region — the late-antique/early Byzantine settlements of the limestone massif that once thrived on olive oil production (4th–6th centuries). By the Crusader period, the region was less prosperous but still agriculturally active. It fell within the northern marches of the Principality of Antioch after the First Crusade.

## 2. Strategic Importance

**Impact: LOW-MEDIUM**

- **Military role**: Part of the defensive screen protecting the eastern and northern approaches to Antioch. The hilly terrain of the Dead Cities region was dotted with small fortifications and watchtowers. Barisha was not a major fortress but part of the network of minor strongpoints providing early warning and local defense.
- **Political role**: Likely held as part of a larger fief under a minor Frankish lord subordinate to the Prince of Antioch. No specific local lord recorded.
- More economic than military significance — olive production and rural agriculture.

## 3. Geography & Nature

- **Terrain**: Karst limestone hills — rolling, rocky, covered with olive groves and scrub, and the famous abandoned late-antique settlements (the "Dead Cities"). Relatively dry with seasonal springs.
- **Jebel Barisha**: Part of the limestone massif of NW Syria, between the Afrin valley (NE) and the Orontes plain (S/SW).
- **Climate**: Mediterranean semi-arid. Hot dry summers, cool wet winters.
- **Agriculture**: Historically one of the great olive-oil producing regions of the ancient world. By the Crusader period, still productive — olives, grain, livestock. The Crusader economy here was based on lordly estates worked by indigenous Christian and Muslim peasants paying rents and tithes.

## 4. Trade & Economy

- Minor node on local routes connecting the Afrin valley with the Antioch plain.
- Not on a major international caravan route.
- **Commodities**: Olive oil, grain, livestock — local agricultural exchange.
- **Market significance**: Low — primarily rural.

## 5. Military Conflicts (1099–1147)

No major recorded battle at Barisha itself. The region was repeatedly affected by:

- **Raids from Aleppo**: Ridwan of Aleppo and subsequent Aleppine factions raided the eastern marches of the Principality. Jebel Barisha was within reach.
- **1119 — Field of Blood (Ager Sanguinis)**: Roger of Salerno's catastrophic defeat by Ilghazi at Sarmada, in the same general region. The aftermath devastated Frankish control in the area.
- **1130s–1140s — Zengi's campaigns**: The countryside around the limestone massif subject to intermittent raiding and insecurity.
- **Overall pattern**: Frontier territory — not the scene of famous sieges, but a zone of chronic low-level insecurity and slow erosion of Frankish rural control.

## 6. Key Characters

- **Bohemond I of Antioch** (1098–1111): Established initial Frankish control.
- **Tancred** (regent 1101–1103, 1104–1112): Expanded and defended the eastern marches.
- **Roger of Salerno** (regent 1112–1119): Died at the Field of Blood near Sarmada, devastating power in this region.
- **Baldwin II of Jerusalem** (regent of Antioch after 1119): Managed the Principality during crisis.
- **Raymond of Poitiers** (Prince from 1136): Faced Zengi's increasing pressure.

## Design Notes

A minor rural site in the limestone massif. Best represented as part of the agricultural hinterland of the Principality of Antioch — the kind of place that generated revenue (olives, grain) but was vulnerable to raids. The Dead Cities setting is evocative and distinct from the fortress-and-plain landscape of most Crusader sites. Could be a "resource" tile rather than a military objective.
