# Cyrrhus

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Frankish/Latin name** | Cyrrhus, Cyr |
| **Arabic name** | Qurus (قورس) |
| **Ancient name** | Kyrros (Greek), capital of Roman province Cyrrhestica |
| **Modern name** | Nebi Huri (Nabi Huri), Kilis Province, Turkey |
| **Approx. location** | 37°05'N, 36°44'E — ~70 km NW of Aleppo, ~30 km N of Azaz |

## 1. Identity & Status

Cyrrhus was a once-great Roman provincial capital and early Christian bishopric (Theodoret of Cyrrhus, 5th c.) that was largely in decline by the Crusader era. By the late 11th century it was under Seljuk/Turkmen control. It sat in the contested marches between the County of Edessa and the Principality of Antioch — the exact boundary is debated.

### Control timeline

| Period | Controller | Notes |
|--------|-----------|-------|
| Pre-1098 | Seljuk/Turkmen control | Largely ruined ancient city |
| ~1098–1104 | Contested Edessa/Antioch frontier | Baldwin's eastward push passed through this area |
| 1104–1108 | Principality of Antioch (Tancred's regency) | After Battle of Harran, Tancred consolidated northern frontier |
| 1108–~1130s | Frankish frontier zone (Antioch/Edessa) | Exact control shifting; contested marches |
| ~1135–1144 | Progressively lost to Zengi's campaigns | Atharib, Zerdana, and other nearby positions fell |
| 1144+ | Zengi / Nur ad-Din | Fall of Edessa ends any Frankish presence |

## 2. Strategic Importance

**Impact: LOW-MEDIUM**

- **Military role**: Waypoint on the Antioch-Edessa communication route rather than a major garrisoned fortress. Ancient Roman citadel may have offered some defensive value, but not regularly mentioned in major chronicles as a held position.
- **Political role**: Historical prestige as former Roman provincial capital and important bishopric. A nominal Latin bishopric may have been established. The region name "Cyrrhestica" was used as a geographic term for the frontier district.
- Overshadowed by **Azaz** (main fortress guarding the northern approach to Antioch), **Turbessel** (Edessene stronghold to the east), and **Ravendel** (important castle in the marches).

## 3. Geography & Nature

- **Terrain**: Rolling limestone uplands and steppe transitioning to fertile plains. Site sits on a plateau/hill with visible ancient ruins.
- **Afrin River** (Nahr Afrin): Flows south through the valley to the west — important N-S corridor.
- **Sabun Suyu** (possibly ancient Lailas): Smaller river near the site; the ancient city was positioned at a river crossing.
- **Euphrates**: ~100 km east; roads from Cyrrhus led to the Euphrates crossings.
- **Amanus Mountains**: To the northwest.
- **Belen Pass** (Syrian Gates): To the west, giving access to the coast and Antioch.
- **Agriculture**: Moderate. Grain cultivation, olive groves at lower elevations, pastoral herding. Historically part of the breadbasket of Roman Syria, but semi-arid steppe by the medieval period.

## 4. Trade & Economy

- Sat near the intersection of two route systems:
  1. **East-West**: Mediterranean coast (Alexandretta) → Antioch → Aleppo, with a northern branch through Azaz and Cyrrhus district toward the Euphrates crossings at Birecik/Turbessel.
  2. **North-South**: Anatolia (through Amanus passes) southward into Syria.
- **Market significance: LOW** — commercial life had shifted to Azaz and Aleppo. Cyrrhus was not a market town of note by the 12th century.

## 5. Military Conflicts (1099–1147)

No major recorded sieges or battles **at** Cyrrhus itself, but the district was affected by:

- **1098** — First Crusade operations around Antioch; Baldwin's detachment moves east through this area.
- **1104 — Battle of Harran aftermath**: Frankish defeat destabilized the Antioch-Edessa frontier. Tancred consolidated.
- **1110–1115** — Campaigns of Tancred and Roger of Salerno to secure Antioch's eastern frontier against Ridwan of Aleppo and Turkmen raids.
- **1119 — Battle of Ager Sanguinis (Field of Blood)**: Roger of Antioch killed by Ilghazi near Sarmada. Weakened Antiochene control over the northern frontier including Cyrrhus district.
- **1128–1130** — Zengi's early campaigns; took Azaz briefly (1130). Cyrrhus area within the operational zone.
- **1135–1138** — Zengi intensified pressure; Atharib, Zerdana fell.
- **1144** — Fall of Edessa. Entire eastern Frankish frontier collapsed. Any Frankish presence near Cyrrhus ended.

## 6. Key Characters

**Frankish:**
- **Tancred of Hauteville** (regent of Antioch, regent of Edessa 1104–1108): Controlled the frontier zone.
- **Roger of Salerno** (regent of Antioch 1112–1119): Managed northeastern frontier until death at Ager Sanguinis.
- **Baldwin II** (Count of Edessa 1100–1118, King 1118–1131): Cyrrhus district was within or adjacent to his domain.
- **Joscelin I & II of Courtenay**: Held the Edessene frontier; Cyrrhus was on their western boundary.

**Muslim:**
- **Ridwan of Aleppo** (d. 1113): Contested the frontier.
- **Ilghazi ibn Artuq** (d. 1122): Victor at Ager Sanguinis.
- **Imad ad-Din Zengi**: Progressively absorbed the Cyrrhus frontier zone.
- **Nur ad-Din Mahmud**: Inherited the area firmly within his territory.

Best represented as a **minor location / terrain feature** rather than a major fortress — part of the contested frontier zone between Antioch and Edessa. For a more significant site in this area, Azaz and Turbessel are the key strongpoints.
