# Alexandretta

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Frankish/Latin name** | Alexandretta ("little Alexandria") |
| **Arabic name** | Iskandaruna (إسكندرونة) |
| **Greek name** | Alexandretta (Αλεξανδρέττα) |
| **Modern name** | Iskenderun, Hatay Province, Turkey |
| **Approx. location** | 36°35'N, 36°10'E — NE corner of the Mediterranean, on the Gulf of Alexandretta (ancient Gulf of Issus) |

## 1. Identity & Status

A strategic port city at the foot of the Amanus Mountains, controlling the Mediterranean entrance to the Belen Pass (Syrian Gates). Western terminus of the great Baghdad-Aleppo overland trade route. Changed hands at least 4 times between 1097 and 1147, making it a contested frontier city rather than a secure rear area.

### Control timeline

| Period | Controller | Notes |
|--------|-----------|-------|
| Pre-1097 | Seljuk Turks | Part of broader Turkish control |
| 1097/1098 | Tancred + Guynemer of Boulogne | Joint land-sea assault; Tancred garrisoned it |
| ~1099 | Byzantine garrison seizes it | Byzantines asserted historic claims |
| 1101+ | Principality of Antioch (Tancred reconquers) | Retaken during Tancred's Cilician campaign |
| 1101–1137 | Principality of Antioch | Continuous Frankish control under successive regents/princes |
| 1137–1143 | Byzantine Empire (John II Komnenos) | Conquered during John II's Cilician campaign |
| 1143–1147+ | Principality of Antioch (reverts) | After John II's death (April 1143), Raymond of Poitiers reasserted control |

## 2. Strategic Importance

**Impact: HIGH**

- **Military role**: Gateway fortress-port controlling the Mediterranean entrance to the Belen Pass. Whoever held Alexandretta and the pass controlled N-S military movement between Cilicia and Antioch. During the Siege of Antioch (1097–98), it was one of two critical supply ports (with Port St. Simeon). Part of an integrated defensive system with **Baghras Castle** (Gaston) in the Amanus.
- **Political role**: Perpetual friction point between Byzantium, Antioch, and Armenian Cilicia. The Byzantines considered it part of their historic Cilician territories; the Franks considered it integral to Antioch. Three-faction tug-of-war (Frankish, Byzantine, Armenian).
- **Cilicia-Antioch boundary marker**: Sat at the geographic seam between Armenian Cilicia (north) and the Principality of Antioch (south).
- **Fortress network**: Multiple Crusader fortifications lined the Amanus passes: Trapessac (NE approach), Calan (N of Belen), Sari Seki (NW approach), Bagras (S route). Alexandretta was the coastal anchor of the entire system.

## 3. Geography & Nature

- **Terrain**: Narrow coastal plain at the head of the Gulf of Issus, backed immediately by the steep Amanus Mountains (Nur Daglari, rising to ~1,500m). Same battlefield where Alexander defeated Darius III (333 BCE).
- **Belen Pass (Syrian Gates)**: Primary pass through the Amanus, located near the town of Belen SE of Alexandretta. Easiest route from coast to the Amik plain and Aleppo.
- **Amanian Gate (Bahce Pass)**: A second, more northerly pass connecting to roads toward Marash and deeper Anatolia.
- **Gulf of Alexandretta**: Large, relatively sheltered bay providing natural harbor conditions.
- **Orontes River valley**: Flows through the Amik plain south of the Amanus, past Antioch to the sea at Port St. Simeon. Alexandretta is on the north side of the mountains from the Orontes.
- **Agriculture**: Moderate. Narrow coastal plain is Mediterranean climate. The Cilician plain to the north was famously fertile (wheat, barley, sesame, millet). The Amik plain (accessible through the Belen Pass) was also productive.

## 4. Trade & Economy

**Market significance: HIGH**

- **Trade routes**: Western terminus of one of the great medieval overland routes — **Baghdad → Aleppo → Belen Pass → Alexandretta** → Mediterranean shipping. This was the primary overland route from the East.
- **Connection to Aleppo**: Well-established road (~100 km) over the Belen Pass to Aleppo, one of the most important commercial cities in the Islamic world.
- **Commodities**: Silk, spices (pepper, cinnamon, cloves, nutmeg), textiles, luxury goods from the East. Local exports: agricultural products. Also handled military supplies for the Crusader states.
- **Merchant communities**:
  - **Genoese**: First Italian merchants in the Principality; received privileges for assisting Bohemond during the Siege of Antioch.
  - **Venetian**: Operated trade routes via Cyprus.
  - **Pisan, Amalfitan, Marseillais, Catalan** merchants also established presences.
- Port St. Simeon (closer to Antioch) likely handled more routine local traffic; Alexandretta was more significant for long-distance eastern trade and as a strategic military port.

## 5. Military Conflicts (1099–1147)

- **1097/1098 — First capture**: Tancred with Guynemer of Boulogne's fleet (Flemings, Frisians, Danes, northern French) captured Alexandretta from the Turkish garrison. Secured a supply port for the Siege of Antioch.
- **~1099 — Byzantine seizure**: While the Crusader army marched south, a Byzantine garrison seized the city asserting imperial claims.
- **1101 — Tancred's reconquest**: As regent of Antioch, Tancred reconquered Cilicia (Mamistra, Adana, Tarsus) and retook Alexandretta. Firmly incorporated into the Principality.
- **1104 — Battle of Harran aftermath**: Catastrophic Frankish defeat caused territorial losses. Byzantines recaptured Latakia and parts of Cilicia. Alexandretta's status uncertain during the chaos; Antioch recovered most territory by 1105.
- **1119 — Field of Blood**: Roger of Salerno killed. Antioch's field army destroyed. Alexandretta not directly attacked but all frontier positions vulnerable.
- **1137–1138 — John II Komnenos's Cilician campaign**: Byzantine emperor launched lightning campaign, conquering Tarsus, Adana, Mopsuestia from the Armenians, and sweeping south. **Alexandretta taken during this campaign.** John II arrived before Antioch (Aug 29, 1137) and forced Raymond of Poitiers to acknowledge Byzantine suzerainty. A joint campaign against Muslim Syria followed in 1138 (targeting Aleppo, unsuccessfully).
- **1142–1143 — John II's second campaign**: Returned to enforce demands on Antioch. Raymond refused submission. John ravaged the neighborhood of Antioch but failed to take it. Retired to Cilicia where he **died April 8, 1143** from an infected hunting wound. His death ended Byzantine control over Alexandretta and Cilicia.

## 6. Key Characters

**Frankish:**
- **Bohemond I of Taranto** (Prince 1098–1111): Founder of the Principality. Captured by Danishmends 1100; returned 1103; left for Europe 1104; died 1111.
- **Tancred of Hauteville** (regent 1101–1112): First captured Alexandretta (1097/98); reconquered it (1101). Aggressive expansionist. Died of typhoid 1112.
- **Roger of Salerno** (regent 1112–1119): Rebuilt frontier fortifications after 1114 earthquake. Killed at Field of Blood.
- **Baldwin II of Jerusalem** (regent of Antioch 1119–1126): Governed Antioch during Bohemond II's minority.
- **Bohemond II** (Prince 1126–1130): Arrived from Italy 1126; killed fighting Danishmends 1130.
- **Raymond of Poitiers** (Prince 1136–1149): Resisted Byzantine demands; killed fighting Nur ad-Din 1149.

**Byzantine:**
- **John II Komnenos** (Emperor 1118–1143): Conquered Cilicia and asserted control over Alexandretta 1137–1143. Died in Cilicia.

**Other:**
- **Guynemer of Boulogne**: Pirate/fleet commander who assembled a northern European fleet; assisted Tancred in capturing Alexandretta 1097/98.
- **Leon I / Levon I** (Armenian Prince of Cilicia): Captured by John II in the 1137 campaign. Armenian claims on the Alexandretta region contested with Franks.

## Design Notes

HIGH-value location. Key design properties: (1) **Contested** — changes hands multiple times, natural conflict flashpoint; (2) **Strategically pivotal** — controls the Belen Pass, the primary route between Cilicia and Syria; (3) **Trade port** — western terminus of the Baghdad-Aleppo route, generates economic value from Italian merchants; (4) **Three-way friction** — Franks, Byzantines, and Armenian Cilicia all claim it; (5) **Paired with Baghras/Gaston** — port + mountain fortress combo forms a "territory pair" that locks down the entire pass system. Works as a territory/asset card rather than a major settlement.
