# Trupesac (Trapessac / Darbsak)

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Frankish name** | Trapessac, Trapesac, Trupesac, Trapezac |
| **Armenian name** | Darbsak, Darbi Sak |
| **Modern name** | Darbisak Kalesi (Darbi Sak Kalesi), near Kirikhan, Hatay Province, Turkey |
| **Approx. location** | 36°31'N, 36°21'E — 4 km north of Kirikhan, in the Amanus foothills |

## 1. Identity & Status

A major Crusader fortress guarding the **northern/northeastern approach** to the Belen Pass (Syrian Gates), paired with Baghras/Gaston which guarded the southern approach. Together they controlled the principal pass through the Amanus Mountains. Located in the "Amanus marches" — the border zone between the Principality of Antioch and the Armenian Kingdom of Cilicia.

### Control timeline

| Period | Controller | Notes |
|--------|-----------|-------|
| ~1098–~1131 | Principality of Antioch | Frontier garrison under the prince |
| ~1131–1137 | Transferred to Knights Templar | Delegated along with Baghras and Roche Roissol |
| 1137–1143 | Contested (Byzantine intervention) | John II Komnenos asserted suzerainty |
| 1143–1147+ | Knights Templar / Principality of Antioch | Reverted after John II's death |

## 2. Strategic Importance

**Impact: HIGH**

- **Military role**: One of the most strategically critical castles in the northern Crusader frontier. Together with Baghras, controlled the **Syrian Gates** — the principal mountain pass through the Amanus connecting the Mediterranean coast (Cilicia, Alexandretta) to the Antioch plain.
- Trapessac guarded the **northern/NE approach**; Baghras guarded the **southern approach**. An integrated defensive system.
- Part of the broader Templar "Amanus March" fortress network that included Roche Guillaume and Roche Roissol.
- A small garrison could interdict major troop movements — essential for chronic Crusader manpower shortages.
- **Political role**: Sat at the junction between the Principality of Antioch and Armenian Cilicia — perpetually contested among Franks, Armenians, and Muslim powers.

## 3. Geography & Nature

- **Terrain**: Amanus Mountains (Nur Daglari) foothills. Rocky outcrop commanding views of approach routes through the mountain passes. Rugged transition zone between the Mediterranean coastal plain (Gulf of Alexandretta) and the mountainous interior.
- **Climate**: Mediterranean on western slopes (wet); drier on the eastern side.
- The castle sits at the intersection of mountain passes — controlling the route from the coast to the Antioch plain and the routes northward into Cilicia.

## 4. Trade & Economy

- **Trade route control**: The main overland route connecting the port of Alexandretta (and nearby La Portelle and Bayas) to Antioch and the Syrian interior passed through or near these mountain defiles.
- Trade goods between the Mediterranean coast and Aleppo/the Orontes valley had to pass through this corridor.
- **Toll revenue**: The garrison collected tolls and controlled the flow of merchants, pilgrims, and military supplies. The Templars were known for managing and profiting from such transit points.
- **Market significance**: Not a market center — economic value derived from controlling access.

## 5. Military Conflicts (1099–1147)

During 1099–1147, the documented events are primarily administrative:

- **~1098–1108** — Brought under Frankish control as part of Tancred's expansion into the Amanus region.
- **1114 — Marash Earthquake** (M7.4–7.7): Likely damaged, along with Baghras and other Amanus fortifications. Roger of Salerno rebuilt frontier forts.
- **~1131–1137** — Templars received control of the Amanus frontier castles (Darbsak, Baghras, Roche Roissol).
- **1137–1138** — Byzantine emperor John II Komnenos's Cilician campaign contested control of the broader region.
- **1135–1150 period** — Thoros II of Armenia contested control of the Amanus forts.

**Later conflicts (beyond 1099–1147, but illustrating the castle's importance):**
- **1188** — Saladin besieged Trapessac. Brutal two-week siege with catapults and mining. Templars negotiated withdrawal, paying 5,000 dinars ransom.
- **1205** — Leo I of Armenia failed to recapture it.
- **1237** — Templars ambushed near the castle.
- **1266** — Hetoum I of Armenia surrendered it to Sultan Baybars as ransom for his son.

## 6. Key Characters

- **Tancred of Hauteville** (regent 1101–1112): Likely brought the site under Frankish control.
- **Roger of Salerno** (regent 1112–1119): Rebuilt frontier fortifications after the 1114 earthquake.
- **Knights Templar**: Garrison and administrators from ~1131–1137 onward.
- **Raymond of Poitiers** (Prince 1136–1149): Delegated Amanus defense to the Templars.
- **Thoros II of Armenia**: Contested the region.
- **John II Komnenos**: Byzantine emperor who asserted control over the area (1137–1143).

## Design Notes

**Tier 1 strategic location.** Must be paired with Baghras/Gaston — they formed an integrated defensive system controlling the Syrian Gates. Together they are one of the most important chokepoints in the Principality. For the Templar faction (if modeled), this is a key holding. The 1114 earthquake could serve as a game event destroying/damaging fortifications. Later history shows the castle was extremely difficult to take by force (Saladin needed two weeks with siege engines) — high defense value.
