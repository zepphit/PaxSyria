# Turbessel

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Frankish name** | Turbessel, Turbaissel, Turbesel, Tel Bascher |
| **Arabic name** | Tall Bashir (تل باشر) |
| **Armenian name** | T'lbashir |
| **Modern name** | Tilbeşar, near Oğuzeli, Gaziantep Province, Turkey |
| **Approx. location** | 37°05'N, 37°30'E |

## 1. Identity & Status

Turbessel sits on a large tell on the west bank of the Sajur River, roughly 30 km east of Gaziantep and 90 km west of Edessa. It was one of the first sites Baldwin of Boulogne acquired during his march east in 1098.

### Control timeline

| Period | Controller | Notes |
|--------|-----------|-------|
| Before 1098 | Armenian lords under loose Seljuk suzerainty | Armenian population predominant |
| 1098–1100 | Baldwin I (as Count of Edessa) | Western anchor of the new county |
| 1100–1118 | Baldwin II of Bourcq | Secondary capital of Edessa |
| 1118–1131 | Joscelin I of Courtenay | Preferred residence; became Count of Edessa 1119 |
| 1131–1150 | Joscelin II of Courtenay | De facto capital, especially after fall of Edessa (1144) |
| 1150 | Briefly sold to Byzantines, then seized by Nur ad-Din | End of the County of Edessa |

## 2. Strategic Importance

**Impact: HIGH**

- **Military role**: Western fortress anchor of the County of Edessa. Guarded the route between Antioch and Edessa. After 1144, the last major Frankish stronghold in the county.
- **Political role**: Secondary (and eventually sole) capital of a Crusader state. Center of Frankish-Armenian cooperation. Site of diplomatic meetings between Frankish lords and Armenian nobles.
- The Joscelins' preference for Turbessel over Edessa itself had enormous consequences — Joscelin II's absence from Edessa enabled Zengi's conquest in December 1144.

## 3. Geography & Nature

- **Terrain**: Large artificial tell rising above a fertile agricultural plain, on the edge of the northern Syrian steppe.
- **Sajur River** (Nahr al-Sajur): Flows nearby; a western tributary of the Euphrates providing water and a communication route.
- **Euphrates River**: ~50 km east/southeast.
- **Afrin River valley**: To the west, connecting toward Antioch.
- **Taurus/Anti-Taurus passes**: To the north, providing routes into Cilician Armenia.
- **Agriculture**: Productive grain-growing region (wheat, barley), orchards, pastoral herding. Part of the fertile crescent's northern arc with sufficient rainfall for rain-fed agriculture. This prosperity was one reason the Joscelins preferred the western county.

## 4. Trade & Economy

- **East-west route**: On the major road connecting Edessa / Euphrates crossing at Birecik to Aleppo and Antioch.
- **North-south route**: Cilician Armenia through the Taurus passes into Syria passed through or near Turbessel.
- **Commodities**: Grain, textiles (cotton), livestock. Transit point for Mesopotamian goods (spices, silk, metalwork) heading toward Antioch's port of St. Simeon.
- **Market significance**: Medium-to-high as a regional market town and transit hub, though more military-strategic than purely commercial.

## 5. Military Conflicts (1099–1147)

- **1098** — Baldwin of Boulogne acquires the town during his march to Edessa.
- **1104 — Battle of Harran aftermath**: After the catastrophic Frankish defeat at Harran (Baldwin II and Joscelin I captured), Turbessel held firm while the eastern county was ravaged. Tancred of Antioch assumed regency.
- **1110** — Atabeg Mawdud of Mosul launches campaigns against the Franks; Turbessel region faces raiding pressure.
- **1122** — Joscelin I captured by Balak of Aleppo; Turbessel garrison holds. Joscelin freed by Armenian commando raid on Kharpurt castle (1123).
- **1127–1128** — Zengi becomes atabeg of Mosul and Aleppo, unifying the Muslim threat. Pressure on the county intensifies.
- **1144 (Dec 24)** — **Fall of Edessa**: Zengi besieges and takes Edessa while Joscelin II is at Turbessel. The defining catastrophe of the Crusader era. Turbessel becomes the rump capital.
- **1146** — After Zengi's assassination, Joscelin II briefly retakes Edessa with Armenian support, but Nur ad-Din recaptures it within weeks, massacring the Christian population.
- **1150** — Joscelin II captured by Turkmen raiders. His wife Beatrice attempts to sell remaining territories to Byzantium. Nur ad-Din seizes Turbessel, ending the County of Edessa.

## 6. Key Characters

- **Baldwin I of Jerusalem** (Count of Edessa 1098–1100): First Latin lord; acquired Turbessel en route to Edessa.
- **Baldwin II of Bourcq** (Count 1100–1118, King 1118–1131): Consolidated the county; granted western portion including Turbessel to Joscelin I.
- **Joscelin I of Courtenay** (lord of Turbessel ~1113, Count 1119–1131): Bold warrior; made Turbessel his preferred seat. Died 1131 from burns suffered during a siege.
- **Joscelin II of Courtenay** (Count 1131–1150): Criticized by William of Tyre for preferring Turbessel's comfort to exposed Edessa. His absence enabled Zengi's 1144 conquest. Captured 1150.
- **Beatrice of Saone** (Countess, wife of Joscelin II): Attempted to sell Turbessel to Byzantium after Joscelin II's capture.
- **Imad ad-Din Zengi** (d. 1146): Atabeg of Mosul/Aleppo. Captured Edessa 1144, transforming Turbessel's strategic role.
- **Nur ad-Din Mahmud** (r. 1146–1174): Completed destruction of the county by seizing Turbessel (1150).
