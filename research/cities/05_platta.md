# Platta

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Frankish name** | Platta, Platia |
| **Arabic name** | al-Blata / Balata (بلاطة) — from Latin *palatium* ("palace"), a common Levantine place-name element indicating an older Roman/Byzantine structure |
| **Modern name** | Likely Tell al-Blat, Azaz district, Aleppo Governorate, Syria |
| **Approx. location** | On the plain between Cyrrhus (Nebi Huri) and Azaz, northern Syria |

> **Note on identification**: The name correspondence Platta ↔ Blata/Balata is strong and consistent with Crusader-era naming patterns. Geographic placement between Cyrrhus and Azaz is secure. Exact site may not have been pinpointed on the ground. Cahen's *La Syrie du Nord* (1940) is the key reference.

## 1. Identity & Status

A small Crusader-era fortified site on the road between Cyrrhus and Azaz (Hazart). Part of the forward defensive network closest to Muslim-held Aleppo.

### Control timeline

| Period | Controller | Notes |
|--------|-----------|-------|
| ~1098–1104 | Loosely under Antiochene control | Following capture of surrounding region |
| 1104–1118 | Shifts with Azaz's controller | Subordinate to whoever held Azaz |
| 1118–~1135 | Principality of Antioch | Roger of Salerno captured Azaz 1118, consolidating the corridor |
| ~1135–1144 | Progressively lost to Zengi | Zengi captured several positions in this region |
| 1144+ | Zengi / Nur ad-Din | Fall of Edessa collapsed the northern frontier |

## 2. Strategic Importance

**Impact: LOW-MEDIUM**

- **Military role**: Secondary fortification guarding the road between Cyrrhus and Azaz. This road was critical because Azaz was the Frankish forward position closest to Aleppo (~30 km north of the city). Any Muslim army moving north from Aleppo toward Cyrrhus or the County of Edessa passed through this corridor.
- **Political role**: Negligible independently. Part of the Azaz defensive network.

## 3. Geography & Nature

- **Terrain**: Relatively flat northern Syrian plain. Fertile agricultural land, drier than the coastal mountains but capable of producing grain. Dotted with tells (ancient settlement mounds) that made natural fortification sites.
- **Afrin River** and tributaries provide water.
- **Agriculture**: Grain, olives, pastoral activity. More productive than the steppe further east.

## 4. Trade & Economy

This corridor was economically significant because it connected to **Aleppo**, one of the greatest commercial cities of the medieval world:

- **Textiles**: Aleppo was famous for cotton and silk.
- **Spices** transshipped from further east.
- **Metalwork**, agricultural products (grain, olives, livestock).
- However, constant military tension in this frontier zone severely disrupted regular commerce.
- Platta itself was too small to be a commercial center — at most a fortified waystation.

## 5. Military Conflicts (1099–1147)

No specific siege of Platta is recorded, but the Cyrrhus-Azaz corridor was one of the most fought-over zones in Crusader Syria:

- **1100–1105** — Repeated skirmishing as Franks consolidated north of Aleppo.
- **1118** — Roger of Salerno captured Azaz, strengthening Frankish control of this corridor.
- **1119 (June 28) — Battle of the Field of Blood (Ager Sanguinis)**: Fought further west near Sarmada, but the aftermath destabilized this frontier. Ilghazi's forces raided through.
- **1124–1125** — Conflict between Joscelin I and Bursuqi of Mosul affected the Azaz corridor.
- **1128** — Zengi became atabeg of Aleppo; renewed Muslim pressure on Frankish positions.
- **1130s** — Zengi repeatedly pressured Azaz and surrounding area.
- **1135** — Zengi captured several Frankish positions in this region.
- **1144** — Fall of Edessa; the northern Frankish frontier began to collapse.

## 6. Key Characters

- **Roger of Salerno** (regent of Antioch, 1112–1119): Consolidated Frankish control of the Azaz corridor.
- **Joscelin I of Courtenay**: Active in this frontier zone.
- **Ilghazi ibn Artuq**: Turkmen emir who defeated Roger at the Field of Blood.
- **Imad ad-Din Zengi**: Recaptured the region during his campaigns of the 1130s–1140s.
- **Nur ad-Din**: Inherited and consolidated control after Zengi's death (1146).

## Design Notes

Minor garrison site, but on a strategically important corridor (Aleppo approach). Best represented as part of the Azaz defensive network. Its game value lies in controlling the road between Cyrrhus and Azaz — the Frankish buffer zone against Aleppo.
