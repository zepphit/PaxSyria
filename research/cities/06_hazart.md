# Hazart (Azaz)

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Frankish name** | Hazart, Hazarth, Hasart |
| **Arabic name** | A'zaz (أعزاز) |
| **Modern name** | A'zaz, Aleppo Governorate, Syria |
| **Approx. location** | 36°35'N, 37°03'E — ~48 km NNW of Aleppo, near the Turkish border |

## 1. Identity & Status

Azaz was one of the most important Frankish fortresses in northern Syria — the forward defensive position shielding the Principality of Antioch from Aleppo. It sat in a contested zone between the County of Edessa and the Principality of Antioch, and its feudal allegiance was sometimes ambiguous.

### Control timeline

| Period | Controller | Notes |

| ~1100–1118 | Principality of Antioch (intermittent) | Control unstable; changed hands with Muslim forces |
| 1118–1125 | Antioch (Baldwin II as regent) | Baldwin II fortified the northern frontier after Ager Sanguinis (1119) |

## 2. Strategic Importance

**Impact: HIGH**

- **Military role**: Arguably the single most important fortress on the Antioch-Aleppo frontier. Part of a chain of fortifications designed to contain Muslim Aleppo and protect the road between Antioch and Edessa. Only ~48 km from Aleppo — a forward position right on the enemy's doorstep.
- **Political role**: Bargaining chip between the County of Edessa and the Principality of Antioch. Its defense often required cooperation between multiple Frankish states, making it a test case for Crusader solidarity. The 1125 battle was a rallying moment for the Frankish states.
- Its fall to Zengi in 1137/38 was a strategic disaster that prefigured the fall of Edessa in 1144 — Zengi was systematically cutting Edessa's links to Antioch.

## 3. Geography & Nature

- **Terrain**: Northern Syrian plain — relatively flat, fertile agricultural zone at ~470m elevation. Gently rolling steppe transitioning to more fertile land near watercourses. The town sits on a slight elevation suitable for fortification.
- **Afrin River** (Nahr Afrin): Runs to the west through the Afrin Valley, a natural N-S corridor.
- **Quwayq River** (Nahr al-Quwayq, the river of Aleppo): Runs to the east/southeast.
- Azaz controlled access to roads running north to **Aintab** (Gaziantep) and northeast to **Turbessel** (Tell Bashir).
- **Agriculture**: Productive land — wheat, barley, olives, livestock. Could sustain a garrison. Part of the agricultural hinterland feeding both Aleppo and the Frankish garrisons.

## 4. Trade & Economy

- **Trade routes**: On the major N-S route connecting Aleppo to Aintab and onward to Anatolia. A branch of the broader network linking Mesopotamia and the Syrian interior to Mediterranean ports. Also near E-W routes connecting Euphrates crossing points to Antioch.
- **Commodities**: Grain, livestock, olive oil (local). Transit trade in textiles, spices, metals moving between Aleppo and Anatolian/Armenian markets.
- **Market significance**: Moderate as a local market town. Controlling Azaz meant controlling (or taxing) the Aleppo-Aintab road.

## 5. Military Conflicts (1099–1147)

- **~1098–1100** — Initial Crusader capture during post-Antioch expansion.
- **1119 — Aftermath of Ager Sanguinis (Field of Blood)**: Roger of Antioch killed (June 28, 1119). Baldwin II rushed to stabilize the Antiochene frontier. Azaz was key to the defensive reorganization.
- **1125 (June 11) — Battle of Azaz**: One of the most significant Frankish victories of the period. Baldwin II of Jerusalem, with forces from Jerusalem, Antioch, Edessa, and Tripoli plus Armenian allies, defeated Aq Sunqur al-Bursuqi's army (troops from Mosul, Aleppo, Homs). Joscelin I of Edessa played a notable role. Secured the northern frontier and demonstrated effective combined Crusader operations.
- **~1130–1135** — Zengi conducted multiple campaigns in the region. Azaz raided and its territory harassed.
- **1135 — Siege by Zengi**: Major operation. King Fulk of Jerusalem led a relief expedition. Zengi withdrew, but the siege demonstrated Frankish vulnerability.
- **1137/1138 — Fall to Zengi**: After sustained siege, Zengi captured Azaz. Part of his systematic campaign to isolate and destroy the County of Edessa by capturing western outposts and cutting links to Antioch. **Azaz remained Muslim thereafter.**

## 6. Key Characters

**Frankish:**
- **Baldwin II of Jerusalem** (King 1118–1131): Victor at the Battle of Azaz (1125). Repeatedly defended the northern frontier.
- **Joscelin I of Courtenay** (Count of Edessa 1119–1131): Fought at Azaz; his county's survival depended on such fortresses.
- **Roger of Salerno** (regent of Antioch, d. 1119): His death at Ager Sanguinis elevated Azaz's importance.
- **King Fulk of Jerusalem** (1131–1143): Led relief of Azaz during Zengi's 1135 siege.
- **Joscelin II of Courtenay** (Count 1131–1150): Unable to hold the frontier fortresses; Azaz fell on his watch.

**Muslim:**
- **Aq Sunqur al-Bursuqi** (atabeg of Mosul): Defeated at the Battle of Azaz (1125).
- **Imad ad-Din Zengi** (atabeg of Mosul and Aleppo, from 1127): Captured Azaz ~1137/38; later captured Edessa (1144). Azaz was a key stepping stone in his strategy.

## Design Notes

Natural candidate for a **major location card / board position**. Dramatic sieges, a famous battle (1125), clear strategic value, memorable characters on both sides. Its fall in 1137/38 is a turning point — the beginning of Zengi's isolation of Edessa. Game mechanics should reflect: (1) its role as a forward position against Aleppo, (2) the need for multi-state cooperation to defend it, (3) the consequences of its loss for the entire Frankish northern frontier.
