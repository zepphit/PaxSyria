> **Note on identification**: Caston/Gaston (Baghras castle) appears separately as #20 in the list, so Yaghra is a **distinct site** — likely a subsidiary fortification near the Belen Pass, possibly the lower settlement at or near ancient **Pagrae** (which shares an etymological root with Baghras: Pagrae → Baghras). Medieval sources sometimes distinguished between a castle and its associated settlement/outworks. The identification is moderate confidence.

## 1. Identity & Status

A fortified site near the Belen Pass (Syrian Gates), distinct from the main Baghras/Gaston castle but in the same strategic complex. The Belen Pass area was heavily fortified with multiple castles in close proximity — Yaghra may represent an outwork, advance guard, or the lower settlement associated with the pass defenses.

## 2. Strategic Importance

**Impact: HIGH** (due to location, even if the fortification itself was secondary to Baghras)

- **Military role**: The Belen Pass (Syrian Gates) was the single most important mountain pass connecting Cilicia to Syria. Any fortification in this area was inherently strategically vital. The pass controlled:
  - The main land route from Constantinople and Anatolia to Antioch and Syria
  - The route by which Crusader reinforcements arrived from the west
  - The route Byzantines would use for any attempt to reclaim Antioch
  - Trade caravans between the Mediterranean coast and the interior
- **Political role**: Part of the layered defense of Antioch's most critical chokepoint. Control of the pass was a constant point of tension with both Byzantine and Armenian interests.
- **Relationship to Baghras/Gaston**: If Yaghra is a subsidiary fortification, it would function as an outwork or advance guard for the main castle.

## 3. Geography & Nature

- **The Belen Pass (Syrian Gates)**: Dramatic mountain pass through the Amanus range at ~740m elevation. One of the most historically significant passes in the Near East — used by Alexander the Great, Roman armies, Arab conquerors, and Crusaders.
- **Amanus Mountains** (Nur Daglari): Rise to over 2,200m. Heavily forested in the medieval period (pine, cedar, laurel, myrtle). Rocky limestone cliffs.
- **Terrain**: Steep, narrow mountain defiles with dense Mediterranean and montane forest. Small mountain streams.
- **Climate**: Wetter than the Antioch plain — significant rainfall, snow in winter at higher elevations.
- **Visibility**: From elevated positions near the pass, one can see the **Amik Plain** (Lake of Antioch basin) — critical for early warning of approaching armies.

## 4. Trade & Economy

- **Trade route control**: This is the key economic factor. The Belen Pass was a major artery of international trade:
  - Silk, spices, and luxury goods between Mediterranean ports and the interior
  - Armenian and Byzantine trade flowing south
  - Pilgrim traffic to the Holy Land
- **Toll revenue**: Castles controlling the pass extracted significant tolls — a major income source.
- **Local economy**: Mountain forestry (timber valuable for shipbuilding at Alexandretta and St. Simeon), charcoal production, some terraced agriculture, beekeeping.
- **Port connection**: Alexandretta (Iskenderun) lay at the western foot of the pass.

## 5. Military Conflicts (1099–1147)

The Belen Pass area saw repeated military action:

- **1098** — Crusader armies crossed the Belen Pass en route to besiege Antioch. Control secured early.
- **1100–1104** — Bohemond's captivity; Armenian lords (Oshin of Lampron, other Cilician Armenians) were sometimes allies, sometimes rivals over Amanus passes.
- **1110s** — Byzantine emperor Alexios I repeatedly demanded return of Antioch. Pass fortifications maintained partly against this threat.
- **1119 — Field of Blood**: Antioch severely weakened. Pass defenses became critical as the principality's lifeline to the north.
- **1130** — Bohemond II killed in Cilicia. Succession crisis and instability.
- **1137–1138** — **Byzantine emperor John II Komnenos invaded Cilicia and marched through the Amanus passes to besiege Antioch itself.** The most significant military event for this area in 1099–1147. John forced Raymond of Poitiers to acknowledge Byzantine suzerainty. Any castle near the pass was directly in the path of the Byzantine army.
- **1142–1143** — John II returned, again crossing the Amanus. Died in Cilicia April 1143, ending the Byzantine threat for a generation.
- **1144** — Fall of Edessa destabilized the entire region.

## 6. Key Characters

- **Bohemond I of Antioch** (1098–1111): Established Frankish control over the pass.
- **Tancred** (regent, d. 1112): Consolidated the fortification network.
- **Roger of Salerno** (regent, d. 1119): Killed at Field of Blood; his death exposed vulnerability of the eastern frontier, making northern pass defenses even more critical.
- **Raymond of Poitiers** (Prince 1136–1149): Defended Antioch against Byzantine and Muslim threats.
- **Emperor John II Komnenos** (d. 1143): Twice marched armies through the Amanus passes, directly threatening this area.
- **Armenian lords of Cilicia**: Roupenid and Hethumid princes controlled territory on the north side of the Amanus — variable allies/rivals of the Franks.

## Design Notes

Even as a subsidiary fortification, Yaghra's location near the Syrian Gates makes it a natural **chokepoint / trade node**. Consider giving it both trade/toll income AND military defense value. The **Byzantine threat** (John II Komnenos, 1137–1143) is the most distinctive military narrative — most Crusader games focus on Muslim opponents, so the Byzantine angle could differentiate northern Antioch holdings. Represents "control of the approach" to the main Belen Pass, creating gameplay around layered defense.
