# Caston (Gaston / Baghras)

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Frankish name** | Gaston, Gastun, Guascon, Gastim, Gastein, Caston — from Greco-Roman "Castron" (fortress) |
| **Arabic name** | Baghras, Bagras (بغراس) — derived from ancient Pagrae |
| **Armenian name** | Paghras |
| **Ancient name** | Pagrae (Greek: Pagrai) |
| **Modern name** | Bakras Kalesi, above Otencay village, Iskenderun district, Hatay Province, Turkey |
| **Approx. location** | 36°25'N, 36°13'E — in the Amanus Mountains, ~12 miles (19 km) NW of Antioch |

## 1. Identity & Status

One of the most strategically important fortresses in the entire Principality of Antioch. Guarded the southern approach to the **Belen Pass** (Syrian Gates), the principal pass through the Amanus Mountains connecting the Mediterranean coast to the Antioch plain. Built on a steep rocky outcrop on two levels around a knoll, with water supplied by an aqueduct from surrounding mountains.

The Byzantines understood its value: in 968, Emperor Nikephoros II Phokas stationed 1,000 infantry and 500 cavalry here under Michael Bourtzes specifically to blockade Antioch before conquering it.

### Control timeline

| Period | Controller | Notes |
|--------|-----------|-------|
| Pre-1098 | Seljuk / local Muslim control | Part of the defenses around Antioch |
| 1097–1098 | Contested | Crusaders discussed using it as a blockade base but chose to besiege Antioch directly |
| ~1098–1108 | Uncertain / possibly Byzantine garrison | Byzantine claims persisted |
| ~1108+ | Principality of Antioch | Tancred likely seized it from a Byzantine garrison during Cilician expansion |
| 1114 | Damaged by earthquake | Roger of Salerno rebuilt frontier fortifications |
| ~1137–1153 | Possibly transferred to Knights Templar | Raymond of Poitiers may have granted it to Templars as early as ~1137; first documented Templar ownership ~1153 |

**Key note**: During 1099–1147, this was primarily an **Antiochene princely garrison**, not yet a Templar castle. The Templars (founded 1119) appear at the very end of the period.

## 2. Strategic Importance

**Impact: HIGH**

- **Military role**: Guarded the Belen Pass — the single most important mountain pass connecting Cilicia to Syria. Whoever held Baghras controlled:
  - The main land route from Constantinople and Anatolia to Antioch
  - The route by which Crusader reinforcements arrived from the west
  - The route Byzantines would use to reclaim Antioch
  - Trade caravans between the coast and the interior
- A small garrison could interdict major troop movements — compensating for chronic Crusader manpower shortages.
- **Paired with Trapessac (Darbsak)**, ~10 miles north, forming an integrated defensive system. Part of a broader Amanus fortress network including Roche Guillaume and Roche Roissol.
- **Political role**: Sat on the border between the Principality and Armenian Cilicia — perennial flashpoint in Frankish-Armenian relations. Whoever held Baghras controlled Antioch's "back door" to the sea.
- **Chokepoint, not a battlefield**: During 1099–1147, Baghras was **never directly besieged**. Its value was in controlling movement. The dramatic sieges come later (Saladin, 1189).

## 3. Geography & Nature

- **Amanus Mountains** (Nur Daglari / Arabic: Jabal al-Lukkam / Frankish: Montagne Noire "Black Mountain"): ~200 km long, max elevation 2,240m. Heavily forested in the medieval period. Major center for monasticism (Armenian, Melkite, Jacobite, Georgian, Catholic monasteries). In 1098, monks from these mountains provided provisions to the Crusader army besieging Antioch.
- **Belen Pass (Syrian Gates)**: ~300 paces across at its narrowest. The most important route between Cilicia and Syria since antiquity (Alexander the Great, Roman legions, Hadrian).
- **Antioch (Antakya)**: ~12 miles (19 km) SE. Baghras controlled the road northward to the coast.
- **Alexandretta (Iskenderun)**: Coastal port to the NW, connected to Antioch via the pass.
- **The Amik Plain**: Fertile lowland east of the Amanus where Antioch sat, watered by the Orontes, Kara Su, and Afrin rivers, with a central lake (Lake of Antioch).
- **Castle construction**: Steep rocky outcrop, two-level layout around a knoll. Water supplied by mountain aqueduct.

## 4. Trade & Economy

- **Chokepoint for trade**: The Belen Pass was part of the Silk Road network — goods from Persia, India, and eastern Asia traveled overland to Antioch, then to the coast. Major commodities: **silk, spices, gold, jade, tea**.
- **Toll revenue**: Could levy tolls on merchant caravans or deny passage entirely. A major income source.
- **Antioch's connection to the sea**: Controlled Antioch's northern route to the Mediterranean via Alexandretta (the alternative to Port St. Simeon).
- **Local economy**: Mountain forests provided timber (valuable for shipbuilding at Alexandretta and St. Simeon).
- **Market significance**: Not a trade center itself — extracts value by controlling access, not by producing goods.

## 5. Military Conflicts (1099–1147)

- **1097–1098 — Siege of Antioch**: Byzantine advisor Tatikios recommended the Crusaders establish themselves at Baghras and blockade Antioch (replicating the Byzantine 968 strategy). The Crusaders rejected the advice and besieged Antioch directly.
- **~1098–1108** — Post-Crusade consolidation. The Amanus frontier gradually brought under Frankish control. Baghras likely came under Antiochene control by ~1108 as part of Tancred's campaign to expel Byzantine garrisons.
- **1108 — Treaty of Devol**: Nominally required Bohemond to return Cilicia and territories to Byzantium. Tancred ignored it.
- **1114 (Nov 29) — Marash Earthquake** (estimated M7.4–7.7): Devastated the region. Antioch suffered major damage. The Armenian monastic community near Baghras likely destroyed. Baghras itself probably damaged. **Roger of Salerno "took great care to rebuild them, especially those near the frontier"** — Baghras would have been a priority.
- **1112–1119 — Roger of Salerno's regency**: Defended the principality against Seljuk attacks. Defeated Bursuq ibn Bursuq at the **Battle of Tell Danith** (1115). Rebuilt frontier fortifications after the earthquake.
- **1119 — Field of Blood**: Roger killed. All frontier positions including Baghras under extreme pressure. Baldwin II assumed regency.
- **1130** — Bohemond II killed in Cilicia fighting Danishmends.
- **1136** — Raymond of Poitiers became prince; stabilized the principality.
- **~1137** — Raymond probably transferred Baghras to the **Templars** to integrate military orders into frontier defense against Zengi's mounting pressure.
- **1137–1138 — John II Komnenos**: Byzantine emperor invaded Cilicia, swept south. Forced Raymond to acknowledge suzerainty.
- **No direct siege of Baghras recorded 1099–1147**: Its position was so strong that attackers preferred easier targets on the eastern frontier (Atharib, Harim, etc.).

## 6. Key Characters

- **Bohemond I of Taranto** (Prince 1098–1111): Founder of the Principality.
- **Tancred of Hauteville** (regent 1101–1112): Likely brought Baghras under Frankish control during Cilician expansion.
- **Roger of Salerno** (regent 1112–1119): Rebuilt frontier fortifications after 1114 earthquake. Killed at Ager Sanguinis.
- **Baldwin II of Jerusalem** (regent 1119–1126): Managed the principality after Roger's death.
- **Bohemond II** (Prince 1126–1130): Killed in Cilicia.
- **Raymond of Poitiers** (Prince 1136–1149): Likely transferred Baghras to the Templars. Killed at Battle of Inab (1149).
- **Imad ad-Din Zengi**: Major Muslim threat from 1127; his pressure likely motivated the Templar transfer.
- **Ilghazi of Mardin**: Defeated Roger at Ager Sanguinis (1119).
- **Nikephoros II Phokas** (Byzantine Emperor 963–969): Built the original fortress ~965–968.
- **Michael Bourtzes**: Byzantine commander of the Pagrae garrison (968).

## Design Notes

**Tier 1 strategic location.** Key design takeaways: (1) It's a **gate/chokepoint** — whoever holds it controls access between coast and Antioch; never directly besieged 1099–1147. (2) **Pair it with Trapessac/Darbsak** — they worked as a system. (3) The **1114 earthquake** is a major event that could serve as a game mechanism — destroying fortifications across the principality. (4) For 1099–1147, this is an **Antiochene** castle, not yet Templar. (5) Three-way political significance: Antioch, Armenian Cilicia, and Byzantium all have interests here.
