# Koumith

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Frankish name** | Koumith, Cumith, Qumith |
| **Possible identification** | Cursat (Cursatum, Curseth) / Qurzahil (Qursath) |
| **Arabic name** | Uncertain — possibly Qurzahil (قرزاحل) if the Cursat identification is correct |
| **Modern name** | Ruins near Kureci or in the hills east of Belen, Hatay Province, Turkey |
| **Approx. location** | Amanus Mountains foothills, between Darbsak (Trupesac) and the Belen Pass area |

> **Note on identification**: "Koumith" likely derives from a corrupted or variant transliteration. The phonetic shift Qursath → Qurmith → Qumith → Koumith is plausible given medieval transcription inconsistencies. The identification with Cursat is moderate confidence. Alternatively, it may be a genuinely obscure site appearing only in fief lists — consult Cahen's *La Syrie du Nord* or Kristian Molin's *Unknown Crusader Castles* (2001).

## 1. Identity & Status

A minor fortified site in the Amanus foothills, part of the chain of castles guarding the Belen Pass (Syrian Gates) and the northern approaches to Antioch. Sat between Darbsak/Trupesac to the north/northeast and the larger fortress of Baghras/Gaston to the south, forming a layered defense.

## 2. Strategic Importance

**Impact: LOW-MEDIUM**

- **Military role**: Part of the chain of castles guarding the Belen Pass and northern approaches to Antioch. Served as a watchtower/garrison point and controlled local agricultural territory. A secondary fortification supporting major castles (Baghras, Darbsak).
- **Political role**: Minor fief held by a Frankish lord or possibly an Armenian castellan (the Amanus region had a significant Armenian population).
- A "stepping stone" in the layered defense of the Syrian Gates.

## 3. Geography & Nature

- **Terrain**: Amanus Mountains foothills (modern Nur Daglari). Rugged, forested mountain slopes transitioning to the fertile Amik Plain to the south. Elevation likely 400–800m.
- **Vegetation**: Pine and oak forests, seasonal streams, terraced agriculture on lower slopes.
- **Climate**: Mediterranean mountain — wet winters, dry summers. Wetter than the Antioch plain.

## 4. Trade & Economy

- **Market significance**: Low. The major trade route ran through the Belen Pass below, controlled by Baghras/Gaston.
- **Local economy**: Subsistence agriculture, goat/sheep herding, timber from mountain forests, possibly charcoal production.
- **Revenue**: Modest income from local agricultural taxation and possibly tolls on minor mountain paths.

## 5. Military Conflicts (1099–1147)

No specific recorded sieges at this site. The broader Amanus region was affected by:

- **1098** — Amanus region came under Frankish control following the siege of Antioch.
- **1100–1103** — Bohemond I's captivity stretched resources; Tancred maintained border fortifications.
- **1119 — Field of Blood**: Roger of Salerno killed. Power vacuum affected garrison strength at minor castles.
- **1130s–1140s** — Increasing pressure from Zengi; minor fortifications vulnerable to raids.
- **1137–1138** — Byzantine emperor John II Komnenos invaded through the Amanus passes. Any castle in this area would have been directly in the path of the Byzantine army.

## 6. Key Characters

- **Bohemond I of Antioch** (1098–1111): Established the fortification network.
- **Tancred** (regent, d. 1112): Maintained and expanded the castle chain.
- **Roger of Salerno** (regent, d. 1119): Oversaw the defense network.
- **Raymond of Poitiers** (Prince from 1136): Defended Antioch against Byzantine and Muslim threats.
- **Armenian lords**: The Amanus region's Armenian population may have provided castellans.

## Design Notes

Small mountain fortress — a minor fief in the Amanus foothills, part of Antioch's northern shield. Gameplay value as a "stepping stone" or supplementary defensive position. Not a decisive location on its own, but part of the layered defense system around the Syrian Gates.
